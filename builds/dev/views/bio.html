I am a copy-editor, aspiring front-end web developer, and graphic designer currently working in academic administration for the Center for Inflammation and Mucosal Immunology (CIMI) at the University of Florida. During my time working for CIMI I have edited a wide array of scientific papers including multiple grant proposals which have won significant extramural funding, and manuscripts which have been published in high impact scientific journals, including The Journal of Infectious Diseases (Oxford Journals), Trends in Molecular Medicine (Cell Press), FEBS Letters (Elsevier), and PLOS ONE (PLOS). I produce all of the promotional materials for CIMI, maintain CIMI's WordPress webpage, and oversee the center's annual retreat.

I hope to transition to a full time career in web development, and have therefore been teaching myself to code for the last 6 months, kickstarted by lynda.com trainings. I have nearly exhausted all relevant lynda.com web design trainings, and my training certificates are listed here.

My introduction to code was Basic 2.0 on a Commodore 64 when I was 11 years old. My father had no interest in paying for a Nintendo Entertainment System when I was younger, since we had all the games we should ever want on that old C64. While I had an interest in programming via my love affair with games, the esoteric world of pure logic presented to me through Basic felt more foreign than CRON.

As valiant as my young efforts in programming may have seemed to me at the time, I never put forward the dedication to produce little more than a very poor, very boring Galaga clone, with an "A" for a spaceship tied to the X-axis and sporting laser cannons that could fire "*"s at any assortment of other letters! Progression from the simple joys of:

10 PRINT "HELLO WORLD!"

20 GOTO 10

to the relatively complex pew-pew of an aerodynamic letter that shoots asterisks did not satiate my desire to create, or prove to hold the attention of my tiny child mind. This would however mark the beginning of a lifelong love/hate relationship with code. I knew that these most mysterious and fascinating engrossing video games could only be brought into the world through the sheer will of the programmer.

By 15 years old I was spending more and more time on the ever evolving internet via the many disparate channels being concocted by unaffiliated individuals. Already there was mIRC and ICQ for accessing anyone and everyone cool enough to be worth contacting. Landlines woke up angry parents and caused for tighter restrictions. So much better the tiny "uh-oh!" to declare that a faraway pen-pal has replied to your 2AM cry of teenage angst and boredom.

Brooding young emotions begot terrible verse which most certainly had to be shared with the rest of the world, or rather those other sad saps in the same sad boats. GeoCities and AngelFire offered budding poets the chance to create a website, for FREE, a godsend for broke, nerdy creatives. The promise was offered that, as long as one could learn the simple language of Hyper Text Markup, there were endless possibilities of information sharing, albeit in a horridly ugly fashion before the widespread adaptation of CSS style sheets.

The period of the internet which saw the rise of CSS also saw the decline of me as a web developer. In the early 2000s my interest in code of any sort dwindled greatly, due to some combination of late high school malaise and early college haze, whereupon I found myself graduating from Florida State University with an undergraduate degree in Modern English Literature, due in no small part to the lifelong love of reading instilled in me through Archie comics and Boxcar Children.

Much of my current work in the Center for Inflammation and Mucosal Immunology at the University of Florida is made up of proofing and editing scientific materials which appear virtually alien to non-specialists upon first sight. The amount of specialized nomenclature and acronym generation in the bleeding-edge science discussed in these proposals and manuscripts which I review force me to work based often more on my understanding of logic and grammar than a more traditional understanding of the English language, a skill which I have acquired through my past coding endeavors. What's more, the majority of my coworkers composing these documents do not speak English as their first language, adding another element of potential confusion to early drafts. I also produce all graphic design related materials for the Center, which can be viewed at my Behance profile.

During June of 2014, I began watching screencasts covering various technologies that the modern internet runs on. After refreshing and updating my HTML and CSS knowledge, supplementing my CSS with Bootstrap along the way, it became clear to me that JavaScript is now the main language behind today's websites. I studied vanilla JavaScript, which led me to studying jQuery, which brought me to AngularJS. It was not until I began to work with Angular that I truly started to understand the mechanisms underlying complex websites and SPAs.

I continue to study and work in Angular, and publish work to my GitHub once a project is functional. I hope to publish many more webapps as my coding skills increase.
